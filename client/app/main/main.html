About Matchmakers

Matchmakers is a serious game ("game with a purpose") that uses human visual cognition in solving “n-way model merging”; a problem that computers cannot efficiently solve. N-way model merging” (nwm) is the process of combining several models of software into one. It is considered an important aspect of software engineering. Some of its applications include combining opinions from multiple stakeholders, and merging products in a product line.

What is n-way model merging?

Here’s an example of an nwm problem:

The following figure depicts three mini models of hospitals.

Hospital M1 contains the single element “Caretaker” with three properties, its title “Caretaker”, attribute “id”, and attribute “office”.

Hospital M2 contains three elements, “Physician” and “Nurse”. Physician has three properties, its title “Physician”, attribute “name”, and attribute “office”. Nurse has three attributes as well—namely, it’s title, attribute “id” and attribute “office”.

Hospital M3 contains only one element, “Nurse” with three properties, it’s title, attribute “name” and attribute “ward”.

To perform nwm on these three mini hospital models, we look for similar elements (i.e. elements that share common properties) across different models, and group them together. Additionally, we try to find the most optimal groupings, i.e. groupings that have the highest degree of similarity between matched elements.

For instance, one combination of groupings would be to match “Caretaker” from M1 and “Nurse” from M2, since they are similar in that they both have the attribute “id”. We can also combine “Physician” from M2 and “Nurse” form M3, since they both have attribute “name”. This combination of matchings is shown in the figure below.

However, this combination of groupings is suboptimal. A better combination of groupings can be made (shown in figure below). The optimal solution consists of matching the two “Nurse” elements from M2 and M3, and then “Physician” and “Caretaker”. In this combination of groupings, the matched pairs share two similar attributes, rather than just one.

The example above is a very small case, there were only three models and a small number of elements. A computer can compute optimal solutions to these small cases without a problem. But once the cases become larger, the size of the problem becomes too big and complex for a computer to solve efficiently. For example, the case we use in this game has a total of eight models and 222 elements. For problems of this size, a computer will take several years to arrive at a solution!

To handle these harder cases, Matchmakers was created, with hopes to leverage human visual processing skills to solve nwm problems and beat existing algorithmic approaches.

The software models in the game are encoded using various visual objects (see the figure below, which depicts the three mini hospital models encoded as aliens). The goal of the game is to identify similar objects and grouping them together. The process of grouping together similar objects in the game will correspond to the process of grouping together similar elements in nwm.
